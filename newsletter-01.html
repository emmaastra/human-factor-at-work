Feature

The 81% gap — why what leaders think and what workers feel are so different

Ask the average executive how healthy their organisation is, psychologically speaking, and 81% will tell you it is in good shape. Ask the frontline staff in the same organisation and only 46% agree. That gap — 35 percentage points — is one of the starkest findings in the European Workforce Study 2025, which surveyed nearly 25,000 workers across 80 indicators.

81%
Executives rating their organisation as psychologically healthy
49%
Frontline managers saying the same thing
46%
Non-managerial employees who agree

What causes this gap? Part of it is structural — leaders are often shielded from the day-to-day emotional reality of work in their organisations. They see outputs and strategies. They less often see the person managing a difficult team relationship while also caring for an elderly parent at home, or the new starter who has not yet felt safe enough to ask a basic question, or the experienced manager who has not slept properly in three weeks.

"The gap is not caused by leaders who do not care. It is caused by distance — physical, hierarchical, and emotional. Closing it requires deliberate structures that bring those realities closer together."
Human Factor at Work · Project Brief · Leicester Vaughan College

The good news is that this gap is not inevitable. The research also shows that organisations with strong cultures of psychological safety — where people feel genuinely included, able to learn and make mistakes, free to contribute their ideas, and safe to challenge things they think are wrong — consistently perform better on innovation, retention, and staff wellbeing. The UK sits below the European average on psychological health at work. We can do better. This project is part of doing better.

Practical Guidance

Five things you can do this week to contribute to psychological safety

Psychological safety is not built through a single policy or training day. It is built through small, consistent actions. Here are five you can start this week — whether you are a manager, a team member, or working alone.

💡
This week's practical steps
1
Ask a question you already know the answer to — in public
Leaders who visibly ask questions — and admit uncertainty — give permission to others to do the same. One modelled question in a meeting changes the room.
2
Name the emotion in the room — without fixing it
"I notice this feels difficult" or "I can see this has been stressful" — acknowledgement without rushing to solve is one of the most powerful things a manager or colleague can offer.
3
When someone challenges you, say thank you first
Before responding to disagreement or criticism, pause and acknowledge the courage it took to raise it. This single habit dramatically changes team culture over time.
4
Check in — not on progress, but on the person
"How are you finding things at the moment?" is a different question from "How is the project going?" Ask the person question at least once a week.
5
Share something you got wrong — and what you learned
A leader or colleague who openly shares a mistake — not performatively, but genuinely — reduces the cost of failure for everyone around them. It is one of the fastest ways to build a learning culture.
Life at Work

Work is not separate from life

One of the most important things the Human Factor at Work project holds is that the psychological experience of work cannot be separated from the rest of life. The person who comes to work on Monday morning carries everything that happened over the weekend.

Our courses address this directly — from balancing caring responsibilities alongside demanding jobs, to managing the emotional weight of significant life transitions, to supporting sole traders who have no colleague to turn to. The human factor is the whole person, not just the employee.

Spotlight Topic

Balancing work and caring responsibilities

An estimated 5 million people in the UK are juggling paid work with caring for a family member. Many do not identify as carers — which means they often do not access the support, flexibility, or legal rights available to them.

Our Crossroads Café regularly creates space for this conversation. And our forthcoming workshop — Balancing Work and Caring Responsibilities — will cover practical rights, sustainable boundaries, and peer support strategies. Register interest at humanfactor@vaughan.coop
