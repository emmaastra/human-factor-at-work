01 — Rationale

Why this brand
looks the way it does

This is not a health and safety brand. It is a human brand — one that happens to sit within the occupational health and safety funding landscape. The design language prioritises warmth, psychological depth, and community over compliance, caution, or corporate distance.

01
Warmth before authority

The teal palette is calm, considered, and collegiate — not corporate blue, not safety yellow. It signals that this is a thinking space, a place of trust, not a compliance programme. The amber accent introduces human warmth: imperfect, approachable, encouraging.

02
Serif confidence, sans-serif clarity

Playfair Display's editorial authority signals that this project takes ideas seriously — but it also has a humanism and warmth absent in typical institutional typography. DM Sans keeps materials readable and accessible for a broad working-age audience.

03
Distinct from LVC — but related

As Miriam confirmed, this brand does not need to match LVC's existing visual identity. The teal and cream palette is sufficiently distinct while remaining compatible in spirit — should materials ever appear alongside each other, they will not clash.

04
Scalable on a grant budget

Every element of this system is deliverable with free tools (Canva, Google Docs, open-source fonts). No specialist print processes, no expensive brand agency. The identity is rich enough to look professional; simple enough to be maintained by a small team.

02 — Logo Options

Three directions for the mark

A core question for this brand: should the logomark be abstract and conceptual, or should it speak more directly to the human person at the heart of the project? Three distinct directions are presented for the Board to consider. Each carries a different emphasis — all are consistent with the colour palette and typography system.

A
Current Direction — Refined

Orbital arcs — Individual, Team, Organisation

Abstract and conceptual. The three arcs represent the three scales at which the project operates. Sophisticated and scalable — but requires the viewer to learn its meaning. Works well in academic and professional contexts.

B
New Direction — Strongest Concept

The switched-on mind — Psychological illumination radiating outward

A human head in profile contains a lightbulb — the moment of psychological awareness, insight, and wellbeing being switched on. From that illuminated mind, three arcs radiate outward: individual, team, organisation. The mark speaks directly to the project's territory — stress, life balance, transitions, inner experience — without a single word. The lightbulb is not about cleverness; it is about the moment something shifts inside a person at work.

The lightbulb

Sits inside the human head — the mind switched on. Not cleverness or intellect, but psychological awareness, insight, and the moment something shifts inside a person at work.

The radiating arcs

Three rings emanating from the illuminated mind: individual → team → organisation. Psychological health is not private — it radiates outward and changes everything around it.

The amber glow

Warmth, not heat. The amber filament and glow rays suggest energy, life, and the human warmth that this project brings to the workplace — across all its diversity of settings.

Strengths

The only mark that explicitly encodes psychological health as an inner, mental experience. Immediately legible — head, lightbulb, light radiating out. Covers the full scope: stress, balance, transitions, workload, organisational change. Distinctive and ownable — nothing in the H&S sector looks like this.

Considerations

The lightbulb is a well-known symbol — the risk is it reads as "ideas" rather than "psychological health." This is mitigated by the human head context and the radiating arcs, which ground it in wellbeing. Professional vector refinement would sharpen the mark considerably from this indicative version.

C
Alternative Direction

Two figures in dialogue — Conversation and mutual support

Two figures leaning toward each other, their heads forming the shape of a dialogue — representing the Crossroads Café, peer support, and the act of being genuinely heard. Speaks directly to the community and mutuality values of the project.

Strengths

Speaks directly to the Crossroads Café, peer support, and community values. The two-colour system (teal figure + amber figure) encodes the meeting of different people and perspectives. The connecting arc and amber dot at the midpoint is a powerful symbol of genuine dialogue.

Considerations

Two-figure marks can sometimes read as HR or mediation sector. The landscape orientation is slightly less versatile than a square mark — works very well horizontally but needs care in square/circular applications. Stronger for the Crossroads Café than for the wider project.

Board Summary — Logo Options

Three genuine directions — your choice

Each option is a complete, workable brand direction. The question is not which looks most polished — all three are equally resolved — but which best reflects what the Board wants this project to say about itself.

Option A
Orbital Arcs

The most abstract and academically sophisticated mark. Communicates the three scales of the project — individual, team, organisation — through clean geometric form.

Choose this if…

The Board prioritises professional credibility and academic register above immediate emotional communication. Works particularly well alongside NEBOSH's formal identity.

Option B
The Switched-On Mind

A lightbulb inside a human head, with arcs radiating outward from the illuminated mind. Psychological health switched on — rippling from individual to team to organisation.

Choose this if…

The Board wants the mark to explicitly communicate psychological health as an inner mental experience — stress, balance, transitions — that radiates outward into the workplace.

Option C
Two Figures in Dialogue

Two people — rendered in teal and amber — turning toward each other, connected by an arc of dialogue. Speaks directly to peer support, the Crossroads Café, and the mutuality at the heart of LVC's cooperative values.

Choose this if…

The Board wants to foreground community, conversation and mutual support as the defining character of the project — particularly resonant with LVC's cooperative identity.

📋
Note to the Board

All three options use the same colour palette, typography, and tone of voice — so whichever direction is chosen, the full brand system remains consistent. The logo choice is a question of emphasis, not a fundamental redesign. A professional graphic designer could refine any of these marks to production quality at relatively low cost — particularly Option B, whose geometric construction lends itself well to professional vector refinement.
