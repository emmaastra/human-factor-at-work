📓 Journal Writing for Wellbeing
Writing as a Wellbeing Practice

Why writing it down helps

Keeping a journal is one of the most evidence-supported practices for psychological wellbeing. Writing about your experiences — especially difficult ones — helps you process emotions, find meaning, and develop a clearer understanding of what you need. It does not have to be eloquent, long, or even grammatically correct. It just has to be honest.

For people navigating the pressures of working life — stress, change, difficult relationships, unclear boundaries between work and home — a regular writing practice creates a space that belongs entirely to you. No performance. No audience. Just thinking on paper.

You can use this toolkit digitally (type directly into the app or a document), or you may prefer a physical paper journal — there is good evidence that handwriting activates different cognitive processes than typing. Both are valid. Both work.

Tip 01
Start with five minutes, not fifty
The biggest barrier to journalling is thinking it has to be a long, serious undertaking. Set a timer for five minutes. Write whatever comes. Stop when the timer goes. Build from there only if you want to.
Tip 02
Don't edit — write first, reflect later
A journal is not a piece of writing. It is thinking. Let yourself write badly, incompletely, contradictorily. The value is in the process of getting things out of your head and onto the page, not in the quality of the prose.
Tip 03
Use prompts when you feel stuck
On days when you sit down and nothing comes, use one of the prompts below as a starting point. A prompt is just a door — you can walk through it and immediately go in a completely different direction.
Tip 04
Try different forms
A journal entry doesn't have to be prose paragraphs. Try lists (things I noticed today, things that drained me, things that gave me energy). Try a letter to your past self. Try a description of a moment in detail. Try writing what you wish you had said in a difficult conversation.
Tip 05
Review occasionally — but gently
Reading back through older entries after some weeks can reveal patterns you couldn't see in the moment. What keeps coming up? What has shifted? What do you need that you aren't yet giving yourself? Approach this with curiosity, not judgement.

🧘 Practical Stress Reduction
When work feels overwhelming

Small things that actually help

Stress at work is real, common, and — within limits — manageable. These are not cures or solutions to systemic problems. They are small, evidence-based practices that help regulate your nervous system, shift your perspective, and reduce the immediate experience of overwhelm.

🫁

The physiological sigh

The fastest known way to reduce stress in real time. Two short inhales through the nose, then one long exhale through the mouth. Repeat two or three times. Your nervous system responds within seconds.

Inhale through nose — short sharp breath
Second short inhale on top of the first
Long, slow exhale through the mouth
Repeat 2–3 times. Notice the shift.
📋

The brain dump

When your mind is spinning with everything you need to do, your brain cannot process or prioritise. A brain dump takes all of it out of your head and onto paper — reducing the cognitive load immediately.

Take a blank page (paper or screen)
Write everything in your head — no order, no filter
Just one thing needs to happen next — circle it
Leave the rest. It is on the page now, not in your head.
🧊

The 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique

When anxiety is high and your thoughts are spiralling, grounding brings you back to the present moment through your senses. Useful in meetings, before difficult conversations, or after a hard moment.

5 things you can see right now
4 things you can physically feel
3 things you can hear
2 things you can smell
1 thing you can taste
🚶

The micro-break

Research consistently shows that brief breaks — even 5 minutes — restore concentration and reduce the physiological effects of stress. The key is to genuinely stop, not to check your phone.

Step away from your screen entirely
Move your body — stand, stretch, walk
Look at something distant (rests eye muscles)
Do not check your phone during the break
✍️

Name what you are feeling

Neuroscience research shows that naming an emotion reduces its intensity. The act of labelling — "I am feeling anxious" or "I am angry about this" — activates the prefrontal cortex and reduces the amygdala's stress response.

Pause and ask: what am I actually feeling?
Name it specifically — not just "bad" or "stressed"
Write it down if possible — even one word
Notice whether naming it shifts anything
🔋

Protect your recovery time

Stress is not the problem — insufficient recovery is. The body and mind can handle significant stress if recovery is built in. Recovery does not mean doing nothing; it means doing something genuinely restorative for you specifically.

Identify what genuinely restores you (not what should)
Schedule it — recovery doesn't happen by accident
Guard it as non-negotiable where possible
Notice the difference in how you feel after
🌿 Getting Outside
Nature, Movement & Fresh Air

The outdoors is medicine

There is strong evidence that time outdoors — even brief exposure to natural light, green space, or simply fresh air — reduces cortisol levels, improves mood, and restores the directed attention that desk work depletes. You do not need to go far or have much time.

Walking, in particular, has a unique relationship with psychological processing. Many people find that problems they could not solve sitting down become clearer on a walk. There is a physiological reason for this — bilateral movement (the alternating left-right motion of walking) supports the brain's processing of difficult experiences.

🌅
The morning outdoor reset
10 minutes of natural morning light — ideally before screens — regulates your circadian rhythm, improves sleep, and sets a calmer neurological tone for the day. A short walk before work is one of the highest-return wellbeing investments you can make.
🚶‍♀️
The lunchtime walk
Even 15 minutes of walking at lunchtime significantly improves afternoon concentration and reduces end-of-day fatigue. Walk without your phone if you can. Notice what you see. Let your mind wander rather than scroll.
🌳
Green space & the nervous system
Time in green spaces — parks, gardens, tree-lined streets — has been shown to reduce blood pressure, lower cortisol, and shift brain activity away from the ruminative default mode network. Leicester has parks and green spaces across the city. Use them.
💨
Fresh air as a micro-break
Open a window. Step outside for two minutes. The change in air, light, and sensory environment is enough to interrupt the stress loop. You do not need a park. You need a doorstep, a window, a fire escape.

🌬️ Mindfulness at Work
Present-moment awareness

Mindfulness is not emptying your mind

The most common misconception about mindfulness is that it means stopping your thoughts. It doesn't. Mindfulness means noticing your thoughts — observing them without being pulled along by them. You cannot empty your mind. You can learn to watch it.

For people in demanding working environments, mindfulness is not about achieving a zen state. It is about building the capacity to pause between stimulus and response — to notice what is happening internally before reacting. That pause is where choice lives.

⏸️
The one-minute pause
Before a meeting, a difficult email, or a stressful task — stop for 60 seconds. Notice your breath. Notice your body. Then proceed.
☕
The mindful cup
Drink one cup of tea or coffee a day without doing anything else. Just drinking. A tiny act of complete presence.
🦶
Feet on the floor
In any stressful moment — feel the floor under your feet. Press down gently. This simple grounding act activates the parasympathetic nervous system.
📵
Single-tasking
Do one thing at a time. When you notice your attention drifting, gently bring it back. This is not a failure — it is the practice itself.
Box Breathing — a 4-minute reset

Used by emergency responders, athletes, and anyone who needs to regulate quickly under pressure. Four counts in, hold, out, hold. Repeat for four cycles.

4
Inhale slowly
Through the nose, filling the belly first
4
Hold
Gently — no tension, just stillness
4
Exhale slowly
Through the mouth, emptying fully
4
Hold empty
Rest here before the next inhale
Repeat this cycle 4 times. Takes under 4 minutes. Works anywhere — at your desk, in a toilet cubicle, in your car before walking into work. No one needs to know you're doing it.
Journal prompt after breathing
"After those four minutes, what do I notice? What feels different, even slightly?"
🤝 Relationships & Connection
The human factor in your working relationships

We are not separate from the people we work with

Working relationships are one of the most significant factors in psychological health at work. Not just whether you like your colleagues, but whether you feel seen, respected, able to speak, and genuinely connected. Poor relationships are one of the top drivers of work-related stress. Good ones are protective even in demanding conditions.

👂

The art of listening

Most of us listen to respond, not to understand. Try listening for one conversation today with the sole aim of understanding the other person — without planning your reply while they speak. Notice what you hear that you would otherwise have missed.

🗣️

Saying the difficult thing kindly

Avoiding difficult conversations is not kindness — it builds resentment and makes the eventual conversation harder. Try: "I want to talk about something that feels a bit uncomfortable, because I think our working relationship matters." Then say it.

🙏

Genuine appreciation

Tell someone specifically what they did that made a difference — not "good work" but "when you stepped in during that meeting, it genuinely helped me." Specific, observed appreciation is one of the cheapest and most powerful wellbeing interventions that exists.

🚧

Boundaries as care

Setting a boundary is not aggressive or unkind — it is a communication that allows a relationship to be sustainable. "I can't take on more this week" is a complete sentence. Practice saying it without over-explaining or apologising for it.

🔄

Repairing after rupture

Every working relationship encounters friction. What distinguishes healthy from unhealthy relationships is not the absence of conflict but the presence of repair — the willingness to come back, acknowledge what happened, and move forward. Repair does not require perfection. It requires honesty and a desire to continue.

🌐

Building your peer network

Isolation at work — whether physical, hierarchical, or emotional — is a significant risk factor for poor psychological health. Invest in at least one peer relationship outside your immediate team: someone in another organisation, another sector, or the Crossroads Café community. Shared experience across difference is uniquely sustaining.
